Redesigning the Engine-Wing Interface Boeing/SCOPE

The Olin curriculum traditionally ends with SCOPE, a year-long capstone project working for an external company. For my Senior year, I worked for Boeing on a project to redesign the engine-wing interface. Unfortunately, the details of this project are under strict NDA... However, I can reveal that I worked as the analysis lead of the team, learned and utilized Patran/Nastran for our FEA work, and played a key role in our design work.

My particular job on this project was to work with a partner to make sure the robot could walk. Bipedal motion is a difficult problem, and an active field of research. In order to avoid complicated dynamic modeling and accomplish a working gait in a three-week timeframe, we elected to use a statically determinant 'shuffle' gait. Our robot keeps two feet in contact with the ground at all times, rocking its feet to contact the front and rear sections of the foot, which have different frictional properties. This motion is achieved via a coupled multi-degree-of-freedom leg system driven by cams.

Our system uses a seven-bar linkage with a rotating foot, all controlled by links driven off three coaxial cams. This system allowed us to design a load-bearing linkage system in parallel with the necessary cam profiles. We first generated a critical dimension sketch to fix design parameters. My partner then focused on designing the linkage system, while I worked to generate the necessary cam profiles. The method I used was one of interpolating keyframes - I build a rough mockup of the linkage system, used Solidworks Motion Analysis tools to generate the desired leg movements, and recorded each of the three cam follower distances at numerous keyframes. This provided the data necessary to generate cam profiles. Unfortunately, this first pass did not function as intended.

Since real cam followers have nonzero radii, the point of contact between cam and follower is not always on the line between their rotational centers. This results in some error, which contributed to very strange motion with the original cam profiles. My partner and I decided to write a numerical method to iteratively converge on the desired cam profile [more later].

24 Hour Design Challenge Personal

One of the first required courses at Olin is Design Nature, a course where students design and build a bio-inspired children's toy. The project lasts about two months, involving a lengthy process of design and iteration. However, there is a challenge among Olin students to redo the entire project in 24 hours - the Design Nature Challenge. In my Senior year, I organized a team to tackle the Design Nature Challenge.

The rules of the Design Nature Challenge are simple: build a bio-inspired children's toy in 24 hours or less. The design constraints are identical to those of Design Nature; teams are limited to a 12"x24"x1/8" sheet of delrin, three servo motors, and a $50 budget. Our design process began at a 8:00 PM on December 16, when the entire team picked an animal motion and a mechanism; we ultimately settled on a sea turtle, and used a spiral cam driving a pair of four bar linkages to produce a rowing motion for the front legs. In order to ensure the system would integrate flawlessly, we took a page from the software industry and utilized pair CAD-ing with a projector. This allowed us to generate and display a critical dimension sketch which was visible throughout the entire design process. The entire design process was captured in a time lapse video, shown here.

Our fabrication process began the following morning. We used a Legend 32EX Laser Cutter to cut the base plate and linkage elements, a Replicator 2 to print the motor mounts and other interface parts, and a 3-axis router to cut the shell. Ultimately, we stole 30 minutes for dinner, and finished the system at 8:30 PM on December 17.
